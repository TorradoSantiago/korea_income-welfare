# Executive Brief

## In one sentence

Applied income-analysis case on Korean welfare microdata, combining machine-learning benchmarks with quantile regression to study how education pays off across the income distribution.

## What problem it addresses

The project asks whether education predicts income, whether richer multivariable models improve that prediction, and whether the return to education is the same for lower-income and higher-income individuals.

## What a non-technical reader can see

- A large, policy-relevant dataset.
- Clean charts and benchmark comparisons.
- A stronger conclusion than "education matters": the payoff to education grows in better-paid parts of the distribution.

## What it demonstrates

- Data cleaning and feature engineering.
- Honest model comparison.
- Econometric thinking layered on top of machine learning.
- Strong fit for analytics, consulting, and applied-economics positioning.

## Current headline findings

- The best predictive model remains a multivariable random forest (`R^2 = 0.628`).
- The OLS model estimates a `12.47%` income premium per education step.
- Quantile regression shows that education premiums rise from `11.46%` at the 25th percentile to `14.17%` at the 75th percentile.
